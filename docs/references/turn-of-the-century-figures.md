To capture the unique atmosphere of the late Victorian, Edwardian, and World War I eras (1890–1920), the historical figures and fictional characters can be grouped by their connection to the era's obsession with science, the occult, imperial exploration, and gothic dread.
## Real Historical Figures## The Occultists and Psychologists

* Arthur Conan Doyle: The Sherlock Holmes author who became a devout, world-famous Spiritualist. He spent his later years trying to prove the existence of fairies and communication with the dead.
* Harry Houdini: The legendary escape artist who used his knowledge of illusion to systematically expose and debunk fraudulent spirit mediums.
* Sigmund Freud: The founder of psychoanalysis. He began publishing his groundbreaking theories on dreams, the unconscious mind, and the "uncanny" (Das Unheimliche) during this exact window.
* Aleister Crowley: The notorious English occultist and ceremonial magician. He founded the philosophy of Thelema and became a dark fixture of the Edwardian tabloids.

## The Scientists and Innovators

* Marie Curie: The pioneering physicist and chemist who discovered radioactivity. Her work transformed modern science, but her glowing, radioactive laboratory notes possessed an almost gothic, tragic aura.
* Nikola Tesla: The eccentric inventor whose high-voltage electrical experiments, visions, and reclusive lifestyle made him the real-world archetype of the mad scientist.
* Thomas Edison: The prolific inventor who, later in this period, explicitly stated he was attempting to invent a "spirit phone" to technologically communicate with the deceased.

## The Explorers

* Robert Falcon Scott & Roald Amundsen: Rival Antarctic explorers whose brutal, fatal races across the barren, frozen, and dark landscapes of the South Pole gripped the public imagination.

## Popular Fictional Characters (Created 1890–1920)## The Gothic and Supernatural Icons

* Count Dracula (1897): Bram Stoker's ancient vampire who leaves Transylvania to invade modern, industrialized London, embodying the ultimate late-Victorian gothic threat.
* The Phantom of the Opera / Erik (1910): Gaston Leroux’s disfigured, musical genius who haunts the subterranean depths and secret torture chambers beneath the Paris Opera House.
* The Invisible Man / Griffin (1897): H.G. Wells' brilliant but unhinged scientist who uses chemistry to turn invisible, slowly losing his sanity and morality in the process.
* John Silence (1908): Algernon Blackwood's famous "psychic doctor" character who uses a mix of psychology and occult knowledge to investigate malevolent spirits and cosmic horrors.

## The Detectives and Adventurers

* Sherlock Holmes: Though introduced in 1887, his most atmospheric, gothic cases—like The Hound of the Baskervilles (1902)—were published and set directly in this era.
* Professor Challenger (1912): Another Arthur Conan Doyle creation. He is an aggressive, brilliant scientist who discovers a prehistoric "lost world" isolated on a South American plateau.
* Arsène Lupin (1905): Maurice Leblanc’s elegant French gentleman thief and master of disguise who serves as a glamorous, roguish contrast to the gritty underworld of the era.
